# Publishers & Catalogue Funds — Constitutional Participant Analysis

## Purpose

Continue the [Constitutional Participant Analysis](constitutional-participant-analysis.md) series — after [Independent Labels](independent-labels.md), [Artist Managers](managers.md) and [Producers](producers.md) — to discover the constitutional role of publishers and catalogue funds, informing POLiPHONiC. Fulfils the highest-value recommendation of the [Constitutional Research Roadmap](constitutional-research-roadmap.md): the first participant type whose core function *is* capital/rights ownership, testing the banked question on administration versus capital/ownership as distinct constitutional categories (CQ-4).

## Scope and Sources

Three institutions, chosen for contrasting relationships between administration and ownership: Kobalt Music Group (administration only, by design — writers retain copyright), Hipgnosis Songs Fund (ownership as the entire function — a fund that acquires catalogues outright), and BMG Rights Management (both models operated within one institution). Findings are drawn from public reporting, sourced in [03-research/notes/kobalt-music-group.md](../03-research/notes/kobalt-music-group.md), [03-research/notes/hipgnosis-songs-fund.md](../03-research/notes/hipgnosis-songs-fund.md) and [03-research/notes/bmg-rights-management.md](../03-research/notes/bmg-rights-management.md). As with the prior three analyses, conclusions here are hypotheses; no new category is asserted from these three cases alone.

## Institution Summaries

- **Kobalt Music Group** — founded 2000 by Willard Ahdritz as an administrative publisher that owns no copyrights; writers retain ownership and Kobalt collects and distributes royalties, built on proprietary royalty-tracking technology. Sold its recorded-music division (AWAL) to Sony in 2021 for $430m to refocus on publishing administration alone.
- **Hipgnosis Songs Fund** — founded 2018 by Merck Mercuriadis as a publicly listed fund whose entire function was acquiring catalogues outright, spending over $2bn on rights from Neil Young, Shakira, Lindsey Buckingham and others. Acquired by Blackstone for $1.584bn in 2024 and folded into the newly formed Recognition Music Group.
- **BMG Rights Management** — launched by Bertelsmann in 2008. Runs an "Artist Services" model structurally similar to Kobalt's (artist retains ownership, BMG administers), alongside separate, simultaneous outright acquisitions of whole catalogues (Mute, Sanctuary, EMI Virgin Music, Famous Music UK) — both models within one institution.

## Human Capacities Publishers & Catalogue Funds Strengthen

- **Kobalt**: a capacity for building infrastructure that serves creative capacity without displacing its ownership — technology and administration in service of, not instead of, the writer's own claim to their work.
- **Hipgnosis**: a capacity for capital allocation and valuation — recognising the durable worth of a catalogue and mobilising the funds to acquire it, distinct from any capacity involved in creating the catalogue itself.
- **BMG**: both of the above, held simultaneously — the capacity to run an administration-only service and a capital-acquisition business as separate offers from one institution.

## Constitutional Functions They Fulfil

The same [Constitutional Functions](constitutional-functions.md) recur — royalty administration, rights registration, synch and licensing — but the ownership relationship attached to them diverges sharply: Kobalt performs these functions while explicitly not holding ownership; Hipgnosis's core function is the acquisition itself, administration being secondary to it; BMG performs both, as apparently distinct business lines rather than a blend of the two within a single relationship.

## Constitutional Contribution

Kobalt's [Constitutional Contribution](constitutional-contribution.md) is administrative and infrastructural — accurate, fast, transparent royalty flow, evidenced by its independent-publisher standing built on that reputation. Hipgnosis's contribution is harder to locate in the same terms: acquiring a catalogue is a capital transaction, not an act of administration or creative support performed for a relationship; its contribution, if any, is to the catalogue's prior owners (via payment) rather than to any ongoing creative capacity. BMG's Mute and Sanctuary acquisitions sit in the same category as Hipgnosis's acquisitions; its Artist Services relationships sit in the same category as Kobalt's — the same institution contributing in two structurally different ways depending on which relationship is examined.

## Constitutional Value Created

- Kobalt: value evidenced by sustained independent-publisher standing and by Sony's $430m purchase of its recorded-music division — external, monetary confirmation of infrastructure built without ownership claims.
- Hipgnosis: value realised explicitly and entirely in capital terms — catalogues bought for over $2bn in aggregate, the fund itself later sold for $1.584bn — with no administration-service value distinguishable from the ownership position itself.
- BMG: value from both registers at once — Artist Services relationships generating administration-based trust and reputation (as with Kobalt), and acquired catalogues (Mute, Sanctuary) generating ownership-based asset value (as with Hipgnosis) — within the same institution.

## Where Constitutional Capacity Is Lost Through Administration

Kobalt is the cleanest positive case seen across all four analyses so far: an institution built specifically so that administration does not become a route to losing ownership — the function is separated from the claim by design, not merely by scale. Hipgnosis shows the opposite extreme: here, "administration" barely exists as a distinct activity — the fund's business is the acquisition itself, so there is no meaningful administration-versus-capacity tradeoff to observe, only a straightforward transfer of ownership away from the people who created the catalogue. BMG is the most informative case precisely because it resists a clean reading: the same institution holds an administration-only relationship with some artists and an acquired-ownership relationship with others, as apparently distinct offers rather than one blending into the other. This does not confirm that administration and capital/ownership are separable, general-purpose constitutional categories — it shows one institution capable of holding both without them merging, which is a narrower and different claim.

## Where POLiPHONiC Could Increase Constitutional Capacity

Kobalt's design — administration explicitly structured so ownership never transfers as a side effect of using the service — is the clearest available precedent for how POLiPHONiC might centralise administrative functions without the capital/ownership risk raised by Braun's and Dre's cases in the prior two analyses: the separation is not merely aspirational, it has been demonstrated as a working business model at scale. BMG's coexistence of both models is a caution against assuming any one institution's structure guarantees which register a given relationship falls into — the same institution can offer both, so POLiPHONiC's own choice of relationship terms, not its institutional form alone, is what would determine which register applies to any given artist or contributor.

## Notes

Findings are drawn from public reporting on three real institutions, not internal operating data, and should be treated as hypotheses to weigh, not conclusions to apply directly. See Constitutional Questions below, and the consolidated bank in [Constitutional Analysis Method](constitutional-analysis-method.md#constitutional-questions-bank), for what remains open.

## Constitutional Questions

This analysis adds evidence to CQ-4 and raises one new question.

**CQ-4 (administration versus capital/ownership as distinct categories):** Hipgnosis is logged as a third, cross-type instance — the first participant type whose core function is capital/ownership itself, rather than a secondary development of managing or producing. This satisfies the resolution criteria's count requirement (three independent groups, at least two of different types) for the first time. It does not resolve the question: BMG's coexistence of both models within one institution is a complicating instance, not a confirming one, and the claim has not yet been sharpened to one precise, falsifiable statement all three instances actually satisfy. Whether to treat this as ready for review is held for human judgment, per [Constitutional Analysis Method](constitutional-analysis-method.md#constitutional-principle).

**New — CQ-8: Does capital-native value have different durability than capacity-native value?** Hipgnosis acquired catalogues worth over $2bn, then was itself sold and restructured within six years of founding. None of the capacity-native institutions studied so far (Mute, Red Light Management, Kobalt) show a comparably fast rise-and-restructuring cycle over a comparable period, though this may reflect the underlying assets' different ages more than any constitutional property of capital versus capacity. One data point; not settled here.
