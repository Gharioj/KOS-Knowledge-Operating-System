# Streaming Platforms & DSPs — Constitutional Participant Analysis

## Purpose

Continue the [Constitutional Participant Analysis](constitutional-participant-analysis.md) series — after [Independent Labels](independent-labels.md), [Artist Managers](managers.md), [Producers](producers.md), [Publishers & Catalogue Funds](publishers.md), [Session Musicians & Sidemen](session-musicians.md) and [Booking Agents & Touring Agencies](booking-agents.md) — as the first participant whose primary constitutional role is digital infrastructure rather than direct human capacity, per the [Constitutional Research Roadmap](constitutional-research-roadmap.md), which flagged this as the highest-risk, most speculative candidate: the first case where the methodology's core vocabulary might not cleanly apply.

## Scope and Sources

Three platforms, chosen for contrasting relationships between infrastructure, ownership and value allocation: Spotify (dominant pro-rata streaming platform, and its Discovery Mode algorithmic promotion feature), Bandcamp (direct-to-fan, high-artist-revenue-share platform that changed owners twice in two years), and SoundCloud (open-upload platform that nearly collapsed in 2017, then redesigned its own payout model). Findings are drawn from public reporting, sourced in [03-research/notes/spotify-discovery-mode.md](../03-research/notes/spotify-discovery-mode.md), [03-research/notes/bandcamp.md](../03-research/notes/bandcamp.md) and [03-research/notes/soundcloud.md](../03-research/notes/soundcloud.md). As with the prior six analyses, conclusions here are hypotheses.

## Platform Summaries

- **Spotify** — the dominant streaming platform, paying royalties from a pooled, pro-rata model. Its 2020 Discovery Mode feature lets a track receive algorithmic promotional boost in exchange for a reduced per-stream royalty rate, drawing repeated Congressional criticism (2021, 2022) and a 2025 class-action lawsuit comparing it to payola.
- **Bandcamp** — founded 2008 by Ethan Diamond around a high, direct artist revenue share on sales. Acquired by Epic Games in 2022 with stated artist-friendly intent, then sold to Songtradr eighteen months later in 2023, with roughly half its staff laid off.
- **SoundCloud** — an open-upload platform that came within months of collapse in 2017 (173 layoffs, two offices closed, debt-funding conditions), rescued by a $169.5m investment, and which in 2021 launched Fan-Powered Royalties, a user-centric payout model routing subscriber fees more directly to the artists that subscriber actually listens to.

## Human Capacities This Group Strengthens

Unlike every prior analysis in this series, the Human Capacities here are exercised once, upstream, rather than continuously in each instance of the platform's operation:

- **Spotify**: the capacity exercised is its product and business teams' design of a recommendation and payout system — set once, then applied identically across millions of streams without further human judgment per instance.
- **Bandcamp**: Ethan Diamond's founding capacity was a policy choice — a high artist revenue share embedded as a default term, not re-decided per transaction.
- **SoundCloud**: the capacity exercised in building Fan-Powered Royalties was a deliberate revision of an earlier policy after observing its effects — closer to an ongoing exercise of judgment than a one-time founding choice.

## Constitutional Functions They Fulfil

Distribution, discovery/promotion, and royalty calculation and payout are the [Constitutional Functions](constitutional-functions.md) at issue — the same functions this series has already examined as performed by combinations of labels, pluggers, radio and accountants, here consolidated into platform-level policy rather than distributed, ongoing human judgment.

## Digital Infrastructure vs. Direct Human Capacity: Where the Vocabulary Holds and Where It Doesn't

The Roadmap asked whether this methodology's vocabulary meaningfully applies to a technology-infrastructure participant at all. It does apply, but with a distinction no prior analysis in this series required: Human Capacity here is displaced rather than absent. It is exercised once, by designers and founders, when a policy or algorithm is built or changed, and then withdrawn from each individual instance the resulting system executes. A stream recommended by an algorithm, or a royalty payment calculated by any of these three platforms, involves no further human judgment at the moment it happens — the judgment was made in advance and embedded in a rule that now simply runs.

This matters most for Discovery Mode specifically. It takes a function this series has already treated as inherently a human-judgment function — a plugger's or A&R's choice of whom to promote — and converts it into an automated trade a track either qualifies for or doesn't, in exchange for a fixed royalty reduction. The comparison lawmakers and litigants repeatedly draw to payola is telling: payola was recognisable, and regulable, specifically because it was a human decision-maker being paid to make a particular choice. An automated system executing the equivalent trade at scale removes the accountable human decision the comparison depends on, while preserving the function's outward shape.

## Constitutional Contribution

Spotify's [Constitutional Contribution](constitutional-contribution.md) is ambiguous in a way none of the prior six analyses' cases were: Discovery Mode's contribution, if any, is to Spotify's own engagement metrics, not clearly to the artist who accepted a reduced rate for uncertain additional exposure. Bandcamp's contribution is clearer and closer to Kobalt's in the publishers analysis — infrastructure built specifically to route more value to creators by design. SoundCloud's contribution is the act of revision itself: recognising that its original pro-rata model under-served independent artists, and building an alternative.

## Constitutional Value Created

- Spotify: value is created for listeners (access, discovery) and for Spotify (engagement, ad and subscription revenue); whether Discovery Mode creates value for participating artists, net of the royalty reduction, is contested by the artists and lawmakers raising it.
- Bandcamp: value evidenced by sustained artist loyalty to a materially higher revenue share, though that value's durability is now dependent on ownership that has changed twice in two years.
- SoundCloud: value evidenced directly in payout terms — Fan-Powered Royalties reportedly increased independent-creator payouts by up to 25% relative to the pro-rata model it replaced.

## Where Constitutional Capacity Is Lost Through Administration

Bandcamp's trajectory shows the same pattern already banked from Wasserman Music's acquisition of Paradigm, applied here to infrastructure ownership rather than representation relationships: Epic Games's 2022 acquisition was explicitly framed around building "the most open, artist-friendly ecosystem," yet the platform was sold again eighteen months later with layoffs affecting roughly half its staff. Favourable terms to creators are a policy choice of current ownership, not a durable property of the platform, and can become uncertain the moment ownership changes — independent of whether the underlying technology or service to artists changes at all.

## Where POLiPHONiC Could Increase Constitutional Capacity

SoundCloud's Fan-Powered Royalties is the clearest positive precedent in this analysis: a platform revisiting its own value-allocation policy against evidence of its effects, rather than treating the original design as permanent. Any infrastructure POLiPHONiC builds should treat value-allocation policy the same way — as something to be revisited, not fixed at launch. Discovery Mode is the corresponding caution: automating a Constitutional Function does not automatically carry forward the accountable human judgment that function used to involve. That has to be designed in deliberately, or it is simply lost while the function's name stays the same.

## Notes

Findings are drawn from public reporting on three real platforms, not internal operating data, and should be treated as hypotheses to weigh, not conclusions to apply directly. This analysis completes the four participant groups originally planned in the Research Roadmap. See Constitutional Questions below, and the consolidated bank in [Constitutional Analysis Method](constitutional-analysis-method.md#constitutional-questions-bank), for what remains open.

## Constitutional Questions

This analysis adds evidence to CQ-4 and raises one new question.

**CQ-4 (administration versus capital/ownership):** Bandcamp is logged as a fifth instance — infrastructure ownership, not just representation-relationship ownership (Wasserman/Paradigm) or catalogue ownership (Hipgnosis, BMG), changing hands as a capital transaction, with creator-favourable terms uncertain to survive it.

**New — CQ-10: Does automating a Constitutional Function preserve its constitutional standing, or hollow it out while keeping its name?** Spotify's Discovery Mode converts the promotion function into an algorithmic trade executed without a human decision-maker at the point of each instance — the same structural feature that makes the payola comparison apt (a human being paid to favour a track) is precisely what the automated version removes, while the function's outward shape and name stay the same. Whether this changes the function's constitutional status, or is simply a new way of performing the same function, is not settled from one case.
