# Human Capacities

## Definition

Human Capacities are the real, embodied abilities of people — creative, technical, relational and organisational — that make any activity possible. They exist in people before any structure is built around them: a capacity to write, to hear, to mix, to negotiate, to organise, to lead. Institutions do not create these capacities; they recognise, combine and direct capacities that already exist.

## Constitutional Principle

No institution possesses capacity of its own. Every institutional function is human capacity organised at scale, and every institutional claim to legitimacy rests on how faithfully it recognises, channels and returns value to the human capacities it depends on. Capacity is constitutionally prior to structure: structure is answerable to capacity, not the reverse.

## Why Human Capacities are the Constitutional Foundation of Every Institution

Strip any institution back and what remains is a set of people doing things they are able to do. A label, a management company, a platform — none of these can write a song, hear a mix, or hold a negotiation; only a person can. Institutions are downstream: they exist to organise, stabilise and scale patterns of human capacity that already exist independently of them. This makes Human Capacities the constitutional foundation from which every [Constitutional Function](constitutional-functions.md) and [Constitutional Institution](constitutional-institutions.md) ultimately derives its authority to exist.

## Relationship to Constitutional Functions

A [Constitutional Function](constitutional-functions.md) is a recurring, nameable pattern of work a system needs done — A&R, promotion, distribution, financial administration. A function is not a thing in itself; it is the stable shape that demand for a particular human capacity takes once it recurs often enough to be named and organised. Functions exist only because capable people exist to perform them.

## Relationship to Constitutional Institutions

A [Constitutional Institution](constitutional-institutions.md) organises functions over time — giving them continuity, resourcing and accountability. Institutions are constitutionally answerable to the human capacities they draw on: an institution that consistently fails to recognise or reward the capacities it depends on is eroding the foundation it stands on, whatever its formal authority.

## Examples from the Creative Ecosystem

- **Independent label** — organises the capacity for talent recognition (A&R) and catalogue stewardship into a durable institutional form.
- **Manager** — translates relational and organisational capacity — reading an artist's trajectory, coordinating other parties — into sustained career direction.
- **Producer** — applies technical and creative capacity to turn a performance into a finished recording.

In each case, the institutional role is simply a name given to a recurring pattern of human capacity in use. Remove the underlying capacity and the role has nothing to organise.

## Relationship to Technology

Technology extends the reach of human capacity and can substitute for some of its routine components — automated mastering, algorithmic curation, generative drafting. It changes which capacities are scarce, in demand, or economically visible. It does not change the constitutional principle: even highly automated tools trace back to human capacity — the capacity that built, trained, directed, or judged the output as fit for purpose. Technology is never itself a source of constitutional standing.

## Relationship to Constitutional Capacity

Human Capacities are the raw material; [Constitutional Capacity](constitutional-capacity.md) is what a Human Capacity becomes once the system formally recognises it and gives it standing — the point at which an ability is named as belonging within the constitutional order, so that it can be organised into functions, exercised through institutions, and later evaluated as [Constitutional Contribution](constitutional-contribution.md). Not every human capacity is yet a constitutional capacity; recognition is the step between them.
