# Session Musicians & Sidemen — Constitutional Participant Analysis

## Purpose

Continue the [Constitutional Participant Analysis](constitutional-participant-analysis.md) series — after [Independent Labels](independent-labels.md), [Artist Managers](managers.md), [Producers](producers.md) and [Publishers & Catalogue Funds](publishers.md) — as the first primary test of individual constitutional capacity outside durable institutions, per the [Constitutional Research Roadmap](constitutional-research-roadmap.md). Every prior analysis examined founders or institutions; this one examines contributors engaged project-by-project, with no institution of their own.

## Scope and Sources

Three cases, chosen for contrasting recognition trajectories: The Wrecking Crew (Los Angeles session players, 1960s–70s, recognised only decades later), the Funk Brothers (Motown's uncredited house musicians, 1959–1972, recognised even later and through external mediation), and Nathan East (a contemporary session bassist whose volume of work converted into recognition within his active career, and into his own group, Fourplay). Findings are drawn from public reporting, sourced in [03-research/notes/the-wrecking-crew.md](../03-research/notes/the-wrecking-crew.md), [03-research/notes/the-funk-brothers.md](../03-research/notes/the-funk-brothers.md) and [03-research/notes/nathan-east.md](../03-research/notes/nathan-east.md). As with the prior four analyses, conclusions here are hypotheses.

## Participant Summaries

- **The Wrecking Crew** — an informal group of Los Angeles session musicians (Hal Blaine, Carol Kaye, Tommy Tedesco and others) who played on hundreds of 1960s–70s hits without contemporaneous credit or a settled group name. Recognition arrived through a memoir (1990), Hall of Fame inductions (2000, 2007, 2025) and a long-delayed documentary (completed 2008, released 2015).
- **The Funk Brothers** — Motown's uncredited house session musicians, 1959–1972, providing the instrumental foundation for the label's sound. Motown credited no studio musicians on any release until 1971. Individual and group recognition (Hall of Fame, Grammy Lifetime Achievement, a Hollywood Walk of Fame star) followed decades later, driven substantially by a book and a 2002 documentary.
- **Nathan East** — a session and touring bassist with over 2,000 recording credits across four decades (Clapton, Michael Jackson, Whitney Houston, Beyoncé and others), honoured by the US Congress in 2007 during his active career, and a founding member of his own group, Fourplay.

## Human Capacities This Group Strengthens

- **The Wrecking Crew**: an adaptable, on-demand technical and musical capacity — the ability to walk into a studio and deliver exactly what an arrangement needs, often at sight, across widely different artists and genres in the same week.
- **The Funk Brothers**: a house-style-defining capacity — sustaining a signature sonic identity (the "Motown Sound") across hundreds of releases credited to dozens of different artists, functioning almost as a hidden institutional identity of their own.
- **Nathan East**: the same adaptable, on-demand capacity as the Wrecking Crew, sustained and self-built across five decades into an individually recognised name.

## Constitutional Functions They Fulfil

Session performance and arrangement realisation — turning a producer's or arranger's intention into an actual played part — are the core [Constitutional Functions](constitutional-functions.md) here. The Funk Brothers additionally performed a function usually associated with [Constitutional Institutions](constitutional-institutions.md): sustaining a recognisable signature across time, but as anonymous individuals rather than a named institution.

## Project-by-Project Engagement: How the Individual Axis Differs

### Constitutional Contribution

A session musician's [Constitutional Contribution](constitutional-contribution.md) is exercised in a single, bounded act — one session, one track — rather than a sustained relationship. This makes contribution highly legible at the moment of the work (everyone in the room knows who played what) but highly perishable as a record afterward: unlike a founder whose ongoing institutional role continuously re-asserts their contribution, a session musician's contribution has no built-in mechanism to keep being re-asserted once the session ends.

### Recognised Contribution & Attribution

This is where the sharpest divergence from prior analyses appears. In all three completed institutional analyses, [Recognised Contribution](recognised-contribution.md) or its absence traced to a discrete institutional decision (BMG's Mute acquisition, OLI's renaming). Here, non-crediting was a routine, blanket administrative policy — Motown credited no studio musicians on any release for over a decade, as a uniform practice, not a judgment about any individual. Recognition, when it came, arrived through institutions other than the one that had engaged the musicians (documentarians, Halls of Fame, Congress), not through the original engaging institution correcting itself. Carol Kaye's 2025 refusal of her own Rock Hall induction adds a further layer: recognition, once finally offered, is not neutral to accept — she objected to being singled out from what she describes as collaborative work, under a group name she disputes. Offered recognition can itself be contested on the terms it is offered in, which none of the four prior analyses' recognition cases (all of which were accepted or self-initiated) had occasion to show.

### Administrative Mediation

Session engagement is typically mediated by standardised administrative infrastructure — union contracts and scale fees, contractors who assemble players for a session — but historically that infrastructure guaranteed payment without extending to attribution. The Funk Brothers and Wrecking Crew were paid for their sessions under prevailing union terms; they were not credited. This decouples compensation from recognition in a way no prior analysis surfaced this clearly: administrative mediation can fully discharge one (payment) while a separate, unmediated, and in these cases decades-delayed process is required for the other (recognition).

### Constitutional Capacity

Because engagement is project-by-project, [Constitutional Capacity](constitutional-capacity.md) here is granted and exercised per project rather than held as a durable, standing status the way an institution-founder's is from the moment they found the institution. Nathan East's case shows durable capacity is not foreclosed by this model — volume and visibility, sustained over decades, converted his project-by-project work into standing recognition within his own career, and into a self-founded institution (Fourplay). But this appears to require an unusually high volume and visibility to accumulate, compared to a founder whose institutional role confers durable standing immediately.

## Constitutional Value Created

Value from these recordings has continued to accrue for decades — the songs remain commercially active — but under standard union session terms, a fee is paid for the performance itself, not an ongoing share of the recording's later success. The [Constitutional Value](constitutional-value.md) these musicians helped create continued generating returns for labels, artists and producers long after the session ended, without the musicians who helped create it participating in that continuing value.

## Where Constitutional Capacity Is Lost Through Administration

This loss takes a different shape than in any prior analysis. It was not an individual founder's bandwidth diluted by business load (Grant, Dre), nor an institution's structural choice about ownership (Kobalt, Hipgnosis, BMG) — it was a blanket, policy-level administrative default (no crediting of session musicians, as standard practice) applied uniformly across an entire era, affecting many people at once rather than being a decision made about anyone in particular.

## Where POLiPHONiC Could Increase Constitutional Capacity

The clearest lesson across all three cases: attribution infrastructure should be a default, automatic part of project administration from the outset, not something reconstructed decades later through documentaries and Hall of Fame campaigns. Nathan East's case adds a second, complementary lesson: durable recognition can accumulate even in a project-by-project model, if there is a persistent, cumulative record of contributions that survives across projects and engaging institutions — something none of the engaging institutions in the other two cases provided or were responsible for providing on their own.

## Notes

Findings are drawn from public reporting on three real cases, not internal operating data, and should be treated as hypotheses to weigh, not conclusions to apply directly. See Constitutional Questions below, and the consolidated bank in [Constitutional Analysis Method](constitutional-analysis-method.md#constitutional-questions-bank), for what remains open.

## Constitutional Questions

This analysis adds evidence to CQ-1 and raises one new question.

**CQ-1 (institutional self-correction as Recognised Contribution):** the Wrecking Crew and Funk Brothers cases are logged as a second participant group, alongside One Little Independent's 2020 renaming. This raises CQ-1 to Accumulating Evidence. The instances differ in shape — OLI's was a discrete institutional decision; Motown's non-crediting policy and its eventual reversal (1971) and the later, externally-driven Hall of Fame and documentary recognitions are a blanket policy and third-party mediation, not a single institution's self-correction — so this evidence broadens rather than sharpens the question. Carol Kaye's 2025 declined induction is logged within this entry as a complicating instance: recognition offered is not necessarily recognition accepted.

**New — CQ-9: Is Recognised Contribution structurally decoupled from compensation?** Motown's blanket non-crediting of session musicians (1959–1971) coexisted with standard union-scale payment for the sessions themselves — administrative mediation guaranteed compensation while withholding recognition as a matter of routine policy, not individual judgment. Whether this decoupling is a general constitutional pattern or specific to how session work happened to be organised historically is not settled from one case.
